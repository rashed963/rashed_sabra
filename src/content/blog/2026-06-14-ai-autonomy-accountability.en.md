Two developments in June 2026 point toward the same leadership problem from different directions.

AI labs are releasing systems that can handle longer, more complex coding and agentic work. At the same time, Argentina has submitted legislation for a new category of "non-human corporation": an entity that could be operated by AI agents or robots, with human shareholders permitted but not required.

One development expands what a system can do. The other asks what legal structure should contain its actions.

Together, they force a question that is more important than whether AI can write more code:

**When an intelligent system can build, decide, transact, and operate, where does accountability live?**

# Capability is not responsibility

It is tempting to describe better coding agents as the beginning of recursive self-improvement. That phrase is powerful, but it compresses several different ideas.

An AI system contributing code to the tools, infrastructure, or models around it is not automatically improving itself without human direction. Today, people and organizations still define objectives, provide compute, approve deployments, control access, and accept the consequences.

The boundary is moving, but it has not disappeared.

This distinction matters because technical capability and moral responsibility are not the same thing. A system may produce a decision without being able to answer for the harm that decision causes. It cannot carry professional duty, lose its liberty, or repair trust.

If responsibility becomes unclear as autonomy increases, the organization has not created intelligence. It has created an accountability gap.

# A corporation without a human operator

Argentina's proposal takes the discussion beyond software development. It asks whether the law should provide a corporate container for an organization operated entirely by agents or robots.

There is a serious economic argument behind the idea. Limited liability made risky investment possible at scale. A legal form designed for autonomous systems could enable new services, experiments, and business models that do not fit comfortably inside today's corporate structures.

But limited liability was never meant to mean absent responsibility.

A traditional company may be a legal person, but humans remain behind its decisions: directors have duties, executives can be removed, owners can lose capital, and individuals can face civil or criminal consequences.

For a non-human corporation, the essential design question is not whether the agent can operate a company. It is:

**Which human or institution remains answerable when the company acts wrongly?**

# The new management stack

As systems move from advising to acting, organizations need more than model evaluations and security controls. They need an operating model for delegated authority.

I would frame it around five layers:

- **Intent:** Who defines the objective, and which values or constraints may never be traded away?
- **Authority:** What data, money, infrastructure, contracts, and decisions can the system access?
- **Evidence:** Can every consequential action be reconstructed and explained well enough for audit?
- **Liability:** Which person or legal entity bears responsibility when the system causes damage?
- **Reversibility:** Which actions can be stopped, rolled back, or contained before impact spreads?

These are not questions for a future in which AI becomes conscious. They are practical questions for any organization deploying agents today.

An agent that can open a pull request needs limited repository permissions. An agent that can purchase services needs spending limits. An agent that can communicate with customers needs disclosure and escalation rules. An agent that can execute contracts needs an accountable principal.

Autonomy should expand only as quickly as the organization's ability to observe, constrain, and answer for it.

# Leadership after execution becomes cheap

When AI can perform more of the implementation, human value does not disappear. It moves.

The scarce work becomes choosing worthwhile problems, defining acceptable outcomes, designing boundaries, testing assumptions, and accepting responsibility for the result.

That is why "human in the loop" is too weak as a governance principle. A person who clicks approve without context, time, or real authority is not meaningful oversight.

Human judgment must be placed where it can still change the outcome: before irreversible actions, at moments of uncertainty, and wherever values cannot be reduced to a metric.

The organizations that benefit most from autonomous systems will not be those that remove people fastest. They will be those that make authority and responsibility clearest.

The question for leaders is therefore not simply:

**How much can the AI do?**

It is:

**What are we prepared to let it do, what evidence will we require, and who will stand behind the decision?**
