I recently listened to a talk about **Organizational Singularity**. Its central idea was not simply that companies will use more AI, but that AI may change how organizations themselves are designed.

The more important question is:

**Are we still designing companies in the right way?**

For a long time, organizations have been built around a hierarchy of departments, meetings, reports, and approvals. That made sense when knowledge was fragmented and coordination was expensive.

With agentic AI, testing an idea may become faster than holding a meeting about it. The question shifts from "Who approves this?" to "How can intelligence flow through the organization with less friction and higher quality?"

This does not remove people from the system. It increases the value of distinctly human work. Systems can gather data, summarize reports, and follow up on repetitive tasks while people focus on judgment, context, sensitive decisions, and risk.

But adding AI to an old process does not create transformation. A new tool placed on top of a slow, approval-heavy workflow is still constrained by that workflow.

The better question is:

**If we designed this work today, with AI as a foundational layer, would we build it in the same way?**

Some steps and meetings may disappear. Some processes may become learning loops that sense, interpret, propose, act, and improve.

Speed alone is not enough. As systems become more capable, governance becomes more important. Every AI agent needs explicit authority and boundaries. Which data can it access? What may it execute? When must it stop? When is human review required?

Governance is not an obstacle here. It is what makes confident scale possible.

For me, this is the work of Product & Technology leadership today: building organizational capabilities that are intelligent, secure, and measurable, not merely shipping features faster.

The next generation of companies will not stand out because of how many tools they use. They will stand out through the speed at which they learn, test, and correct, and through the clarity of where human judgment must remain decisive.

It is therefore not enough to ask about an organization's AI strategy. The deeper question is:

**Is the organization itself designed to learn?**
